# Presentation Script · Dieter Rams Tribute
**A talking-points walkthrough for the recorded video presentation**

Max McDonough · INTD-215 Designing for Screens · Spring 2026 · Instructor: Colby May

---

## How to use this script

This is structured as a per-page script with talking points, on-screen actions, and approximate timings. Total run-time target: **6–8 minutes**.

Each section has:
- **What to show** — what's on screen at that moment
- **What to say** — the actual narration
- **Timing** — rough seconds

Pace it however feels natural. The brackets `[ ]` are stage directions, not lines to read.

---

## 0:00 — OPEN (~30 seconds)

**What to show:** The cover page — `index.html`. Hero with Dieter Rams's name in the Braun device frame.

**What to say:**
> "Hi. I'm Max McDonough. This is my final project for Designing for Screens — a six-page editorial tribute to Dieter Rams, the German industrial designer who spent forty-two years at Braun and wrote the ten principles of good design in 1976.
>
> I picked Rams because his work is the closest the field of industrial design has ever come to an ethical compass. Every product in your pocket — your phone, your calculator app, your headphones — traces back to him through Apple's Jonathan Ive, who's cited Rams as his single greatest influence for two decades.
>
> The site I built isn't only documentation. It tries to embody the principles instead of just listing them. Let me walk you through the chapters."

[Wait two beats, then click into Chapter I]

---

## 0:30 — CHAPTER I · BIOGRAPHY (~1 minute)

**What to show:** `about.html`. Scroll down past the chapter intro card to the biography portrait + meta block, then to the Braun T 1000 dial.

**What to say:**
> "Chapter One is biography. The first half is a traditional editorial layout — portrait on the left, biographical metadata in a small label list, and a four-paragraph bio on the right.
>
> The second half is the timeline." [Scroll into the dial.] "I rebuilt the conventional vertical-with-dot-marker timeline as the tuning panel of a Braun T 1000 World Receiver. As you scroll, the year rotor slides under a fixed orange marker. The knob spins thirty-six degrees per tick. The readout panel cross-fades the year, the event title in serif italic, and the body text.
>
> Why a dial instead of a list? Because reading a chronology by year-event-year-event is exactly how a tuner panel works. The user is already trained for that interface. The metaphor isn't decoration — it's recognition."

[Scroll through a couple of years to demonstrate.]

---

## 1:30 — CHAPTER II · PRINCIPLES (~1 minute)

**What to show:** `principles.html`. Show the calculator. Click a few keys. Press the orange equals key.

**What to say:**
> "Chapter Two is the ten principles. This was the hardest page in the project. The principles are the most-quoted text in industrial design — every Rams tribute lays them out as a numbered list. I needed a treatment that wasn't a list.
>
> The Braun ET 66 calculator solved it. Ten principles, ten keys. The display panel above shows the active principle's number in huge orange numerals, the title in serif italic, and the body copy. You can click a key, hover, or press 1 through 9 on your keyboard.
>
> Principle 10 — 'Less, but better' — sits in the orange equals position." [Click the orange key.] "Because the equals key is where the calculator says 'this is the answer.' That joke wrote itself."

[Linger on the orange display.]

---

## 2:30 — CHAPTER III · WORK (~1 minute)

**What to show:** `work.html`. Scroll through 2–3 product features.

**What to say:**
> "Chapter Three is the work — nine objects in detail, in chronological order from the SK 4 Phonosuper in 1956 to the ET 66 Calculator in 1987.
>
> Originally I had this as a 3-by-3 grid of cards with All, Braun, and Vitsœ filter buttons. I replaced it with this parallax scroll because cards in a grid all read as equivalent — and these objects aren't equivalent. Each one solved a specific design problem and deserves a paragraph explaining what.
>
> Each row alternates left-right." [Scroll past a row to demonstrate.] "Notice the copy slides up and the image clip-reveals from left to right when the row enters view. Below the body text is a four-cell technical spec — material, production years, innovation, where it's collected — because Rams's actual Braun catalogues did exactly this kind of plain typographic spec sheet."

[Scroll through one or two more.]

---

## 3:30 — CHAPTER IV · IMPACT (~1.5 minutes)

**What to show:** `impact.html`. Three sections in order: meters, hi-fi rack, designers gallery.

**What to say:**
> "Chapter Four is impact — the bottom line. Three different Braun-instrument metaphors on a single page because they all answer the same question: what does the work add up to?
>
> First, the studio meters." [Scroll to meters.] "Six purpose-built readouts. The first version of this used six identical VU dials, but they implied false comparison — '1932' and '42' looked the same on the same scale. So I rebuilt it with the right instrument per data type. A date pad for Born. A linear timeline bar for Tenure. A row of ten LEDs lighting up sequentially for Principles. A model-number plate for the Vitsœ 606. An odometer that rolls for Products Designed. A histogram for Museum Collections.
>
> Next, the hi-fi rack." [Scroll.] "Six channels broadcasting Rams's legacy: Apple-and-Ive, Museum Collections, the Rams documentary, Education, Sustainability, Vitsœ. Each module has its own LED VU bar showing 'ten of ten, LIVE' because every one of these channels is still active.
>
> Finally, Designers He Shaped." [Scroll into the influence section.] "A sticky left pane with the designer's name rotating character-by-character, synced to scrolling cards on the right. Seven contemporary designers Rams influenced — Jonathan Ive, Naoto Fukasawa, Jasper Morrison, Marc Newson, Sam Hecht, Konstantin Grcic, Hella Jongerius — each with a hand-drawn SVG silhouette of their signature work. Ive's iPhone, Fukasawa's MUJI CD player, Morrison's Air Chair, Newson's Apple Watch, and so on."

[Scroll through 2–3 designer cards.]

---

## 5:00 — CHAPTER V · INVITATION (~1 minute)

**What to show:** `invitation.html`. The Eleventh Principle form. Type a word. Submit. Show the result panel and closing mantra.

**What to say:**
> "Chapter Five is the closing move. The site's final question.
>
> Rams stopped at ten. The framework was complete in 1976. But every designer who works with it long enough eventually adds their own. So this page asks the visitor for theirs — in one word." [Type a word, e.g. "alive".] "The counter forces restraint — fourteen characters max, turning warning at twelve and red at fourteen. One word. Less, but better.
>
> Submit." [Click submit.] "And your principle is filed. Stamped, dated, given a record number, copied to your clipboard. The visitor leaves having added to the framework instead of just reading it.
>
> Below that is the closing mantra." [Scroll down.] "Full-bleed black, the German on top in sans-light, the English in serif italic accent-orange. Like the last frame of a documentary."

---

## 6:00 — THE EVOLUTION (~45 seconds)

**What to show:** Switch tabs to the process book OR pull up the snapshots folder.

**What to say:**
> "Quick word on how the site got here. The repository preserves four evolution snapshots.
>
> Version 1 was the original — clean, hand-coded HTML, CSS, vanilla JavaScript, no AI assistance, no plugins. A single-page tribute that worked but didn't yet embody the principles.
>
> Version 2 added four open-source React components from 21st-dot-dev — the dot-pattern quote, parallax scroll, wave path, and text-rotate gallery — each adapted from React-Tailwind into vanilla to fit the host.
>
> Version 3 was the deep build-out with Claude Code. Every section got rebuilt around an actual Braun product as its interface metaphor — the calculator, the dial, the meter rack, the hi-fi rack.
>
> Version 4, the version you're looking at now, was the multi-page restructure. Colby May's note in critique was the most Rams-true feedback I got: a single endless scroll inherently violates 'less, but better.' Multi-page focused chapters honour the principle directly. So the whole site got split into a cover plus five chapters."

---

## 6:45 — CLOSING (~30 seconds)

**What to show:** Return to cover page. Maybe linger on the closing mantra panel of the invitation page.

**What to say:**
> "The thesis of the project is simple. Good design is testable. If the work is the proof of the principles, the medium that documents the work should be the same proof — scaled to the screen.
>
> Less, but better. Thank you."

[Hold for two beats. End.]

## TECHNICAL NOTES FOR RECORDING

- **Browser:** Use Chrome or Firefox in private/incognito mode so you don't have any extension UI showing.
- **Window size:** 1440 × 900 minimum. Full-screen the browser if possible.
- **Cursor:** Move slowly. Pause on interactive elements for half a second before clicking so the viewer can see what's about to happen.
- **Audio:** Record voice separately and overlay if possible — much cleaner than browser-mic capture.
- **Length:** Aim for 6–8 minutes. Anything past 10 starts to drag for a class presentation.
- **Pre-record the calculator demo** in particular: hover keys, click a few, end on the orange equals. Same for the dial — scroll smoothly through 4–5 years, don't rush.

## OPTIONAL ADDITIONAL TALKING POINTS (if time permits)

- **The persistent HUD** at the bottom of every page: lowercase braun · current chapter · italic chapter name · scroll progress · pulsing LED · REC. It's the tape-deck readout. Knits the whole site together as one continuous instrument.
- **The chapter rail** on the right edge of every page: six dots, current one in orange. The site index, always available.
- **Reduced-motion respect:** every animation has a media-query override. Press the user's OS-level "reduce motion" setting and every transition collapses to instant.
- **Print stylesheet:** the principles can be printed cleanly from any chapter page.
- **The whole thing is vanilla:** no React, no Vue, no Tailwind, no jQuery, no build step. Three files plus images. Opens directly in any browser. That's a deliberate choice in keeping with Rams's principle 7: long-lasting.
